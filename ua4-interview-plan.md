## Open-ended phase guide

Question areas for the problem-discovery half of the interview. Ordered roughly broad to specific. Two or three example phrasings per area. **Bullet points, not a script.**

> Reminders:
>
> - Ask about past behavior, not hypothetical future behavior. "When was the last time…" beats "would you ever…".
> - Don't ask hypothetical, feature, or validation questions. They produce nodding, not learning.
> - Don't pitch your idea in this phase.
> - Let silence be okay.

- **Their current eating habits**
  - "Walk me through what you ate yesterday — how did you decide what to have?"
  - "On a normal day during your season / training week, how do you approach meals?"

- **Awareness of a nutrition gap**
  - "When was the last time you thought about whether you were eating the right things? What was going on?"
  - "Has anyone ever told you what you should be eating for your sport/training? What did they say?"

- **Performance and goals**
  - "What are you trying to get out of all the training you're doing right now?"
  - "Has your diet ever felt like it was holding you back from something? Walk me through that."

- **Past attempts to figure it out**
  - "Have you ever looked something up to figure out if a food was good for your workouts? What happened?"
  - "Have you ever used an app or website to track what you eat — what was that like?"
  - "If you realized you weren't eating the right stuff, what would you actually do about it?"

- **What they do when confused**
  - "When you're not sure if you're eating well enough, what do you do — do you ask someone, ignore it, try to figure it out?"

## Falsification commitments

**One set per planned interview. Written before the interview happens. Reviewed and updated after.**

### Interview 1 — Jayden (school basketball team)

#### Need or opportunity

- **Current belief:** Athletes know they need to eat better but have no clear roadmap — all they hear is generic advice like "eat more protein and vegetables"
- **Confirms it if:** Jayden can't name what he's specifically supposed to eat for basketball, or says his only guidance is vague
- **Refutes it if:** Jayden has a real nutrition plan from a coach, dietitian, or app he's already happy with
- **Complicates it if:** He gets some guidance from his coach but still feels like it's not specific enough
- **What actually happened:** CONFIRMED. Coach told him to "eat a lot of carbs" but gave no specific foods — just "don't eat fast food before a game." He described waking up with a cold sweat after eating badly the day before, and actively trying to course-correct. Clear evidence the problem is real and felt.

#### Target customer

- **Current belief:** Competitive athletes working toward a specific weight or performance goal
- **Confirms it if:** Jayden has a concrete goal (performance, body composition, energy) that his eating is connected to
- **Refutes it if:** He doesn't think about eating in relation to basketball at all — food and sport are completely separate in his mind
- **Complicates it if:** He has a goal but it's not connected to nutrition — he just trains harder instead of eating differently
- **What actually happened:** CONFIRMED AND EXPANDED. Jayden clearly connects eating to performance ("you need to know what you are eating before your game so you don't perform poorly"). Target customer refined from "competitive athletes" to "athletes and people working toward better athleticism" — the Jayden interview revealed the real issue is apps built for weight loss, not performance.

#### Primary competitive alternative

- **Current belief:** Athletes are currently winging it or relying on generic advice — not using a dedicated app or plan
- **Confirms it if:** Jayden isn't using any app or plan and has no real system for his nutrition
- **Refutes it if:** He's already using an app he likes and feels it's working
- **Complicates it if:** He tried an app before but stopped — worth finding out why
- **What actually happened:** COMPLICATED — and revealing. He used an app in middle school but quit because it told him he was overweight, which "hurt his feelings." The real problem isn't paywalls — it's that existing apps are built for weight loss, not athletic performance. Current workaround: "just keep eating." Inertia is the real competitor.
